"Among the 250 species of turtles on Earth, seven of them live in the ocean (called sea turtles), and the remaining 243 species live on land (terrestrial turtles called tortoises) or in freshwater ponds and marshes. Five of the seven species of sea turtles – the Green Turtle (Chelonia mydas), Hawksbill Turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata), Olive Ridley (Lepidochelys olivacea), Loggerhead (Caretta caretta) and Leatherback (Dermochelys coriacea) – visit the Sri Lankan shores to nest."

Turtles found in srilanka

Green Turtle

Green turtles have an average length of 1 metre and can weigh up to 230 kg. They are migratory and found in all tropical and sub-tropical seas. They are caught and killed to make 'turtle soup', which is a delicacy. Young green turtles are carnivorous, while adults are herbivorous. They are now considered endangered.

Hawksbill Turtle

Hawksbill turtles are small turtles that inhabit tropical coastal waters and are mostly carnivorous. They have narrow birdlike beaks and can store toxic sponges in their own flesh. They are now highly endangered due to people killing them for their shell, which is used to make 'tortoiseshell' ornaments.

Leather back Turtle

The Leatherback turtle is the largest marine turtle and can grow up to 2 metres in length and weigh 600 kg. They have a predominant dorsal black colour with variable degrees of white or paler spotting. They feed exclusively on jellyfish and travel long distances in search of their prey. They can survive in the extreme cold due to their unique carapace and can dive to depths of 1500 metres in search of deep-sea jellyfish. Today, they are considered critically endangered.

Logger Head Turtle

Loggerhead turtles are one of the commonest species in the world, but are the most rare nesting species in Sri Lanka. They are primarily carnivorous and feed on mollusks and crustaceans, and their unique colouration suggests a unique population with unique genes. It is important to take immediate conservation measures to protect Loggerhead turtles in Sri Lanka.

Olive ridely Turtle

Olive Ridley turtles are omnivorous and found in the tropical Indian, south Atlantic and Pacific Oceans. They are considered endangered due to their large populations, which depend on the security of a small number of important beaches for nesting. Arribada is a Spanish word meaning "the arrival" and is used to refer to a large congregation of Olive Ridley turtles nesting on a beach.
